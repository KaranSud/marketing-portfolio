Most cold emails fail for the same reason. They are written about the sender, not the reader. The fix is not a clever template or a new subject-line hack. It is a shift in who the email is actually about.

## Why the average cold email gets deleted

Open a typical outbound inbox and the pattern repeats. A greeting, a paragraph about the sender's company, a list of features, and a request for thirty minutes. The reader learns nothing about themselves and is asked to give up time before they have any reason to. Delete.

People reply to messages that prove the sender did their homework and respect their time. Everything below serves those two goals.

## The four parts that actually work

**One specific observation about them.** Open with something real you noticed: a product they shipped, a page on their site, a role they are hiring for, a recent announcement. This is the single highest-leverage line in the email, because it proves the message was written for one person, not blasted to a thousand.

**One reason it is relevant right now.** Connect that observation to a problem they plausibly have. Not your features. The situation they are likely in, and why this is the moment it matters.

**One low-friction ask.** Skip the thirty-minute meeting request. Ask a question that costs almost nothing to answer: "worth a quick look?", "want me to send a two-line idea?", "open to a short reply?". The goal of the first email is a reply, not a calendar invite.

**Nothing else.** No feature list, no company history, no second ask. Length is a tax on the reader. Fifty to ninety words is plenty.

## What to leave out

- "I hope this email finds you well." It tells the reader nothing and signals a template.
- Fake compliments. People can feel the difference between research and flattery.
- A wall of social proof. One relevant proof point beats five logos.
- A hard close. Pressure in a first touch reads as desperation.

## The honesty test

Before you send, read the email as the recipient. Could this exact message have gone to a hundred other companies? If yes, it is not ready. Change the opening line until it could only have been written to them.

That is the whole craft. Be specific, be relevant, be brief, and make the next step easy. The structure is simple. The discipline of doing it for every account is the hard part, and it is also the part that works.
