Almost every marketing playbook online quietly assumes you sell software. Product-led growth, free trials, developer content, activation metrics. None of that maps cleanly to a restaurant, a furniture brand, a dental group, or a plumber. The fundamentals still hold, but what you measure and where you spend changes completely.

## Different business, different scoreboard

For a SaaS company the scoreboard is signups, activation, and retention. For other businesses the numbers that matter look nothing like that.

- A **restaurant** lives on covers, delivery orders, reviews, and how full the room is on a Tuesday.
- An **e-commerce brand** lives on conversion rate, average order value, return on ad spend, and repeat purchase rate.
- A **local service** lives on calls booked, jobs won, service-area coverage, and word of mouth.
- A **professional firm** lives on qualified inquiries, referrals, and the quality of the clients it closes.

If you bring SaaS language to these businesses, you will optimize the wrong thing. The first job is to learn their real scoreboard and speak in it.

## Where attention actually comes from

Software buyers are often online in predictable places. Other businesses pull demand from channels a tech marketer can overlook.

- **Local search and maps.** For anyone with a location, showing up when someone nearby searches is the whole game. Reviews and an accurate profile do more than a clever campaign.
- **Reputation.** A steady flow of recent, genuine reviews is the single strongest growth lever for restaurants, clinics, and trades.
- **Visual proof.** Food, interiors, before-and-after work, and finished products sell themselves when shown well. The content is the storefront.
- **Community and referral.** A regular at a cafe or a happy client of a firm is a marketing channel, not just a customer.

## What stays exactly the same

The surface changes, but the spine of good marketing does not. You still start from a sharp idea of who you serve. You still lead with a specific, relevant message instead of a generic one. You still respect people's time and attention. You still measure what you spend against what comes back.

The mistake is assuming the tactics that worked for a developer tool will work for a bakery. The skill is keeping the principles and rebuilding the tactics for the business in front of you. Do that, and the same operator who can grow a SaaS can grow a restaurant down the street.
