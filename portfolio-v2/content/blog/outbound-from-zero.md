Most outbound advice assumes you already have a list. A new brand rarely does. When a company hands me a fresh product and says go find customers, there is no CRM, no enriched contacts, and no past campaigns to learn from. You start from nothing. Here is the sequence I actually run.

## Start with the offer, not the list

Before you look for a single company, get painfully clear on what you sell and who it changes the day for. Write it in one or two sentences a stranger could repeat back. If you cannot, the list will be noise no matter how big it is. The offer defines the customer, so the offer comes first.

From that offer you can write the profile of the account that should care: the industry, the rough size and stage, the region, and the moment that makes them ready to buy. That moment matters more than the firmographics. A company hiring its first growth lead, launching in a new market, or raising a round is a company with a reason to talk this month.

## Build the first list from open data

You do not need a paid database to get started. The open web holds more than enough to fill a first pipeline.

- Public company graphs like Wikidata let you pull real companies by industry, each with a verified website. That alone can seed a few dozen accounts in minutes.
- Directories and marketplaces are lists in disguise. Crunchbase, Product Hunt, G2, and industry association pages all publish companies you can work through by hand.
- Communities tell you where your buyers gather. A focused subreddit, a Slack group, or a niche conference attendee list is a warmer source than any scraped export.
- Job boards are intent signals. A company hiring for the exact problem you solve is telling you they care about it right now.

The goal of the first list is not scale. It is relevance. Forty accounts you understand will beat four thousand you do not.

## Research before you write

Spraying the same email at a list is the fastest way to get filtered. Before you write a word, read the account. What do they actually do, what are they focused on this quarter, and where does your offer plausibly fit their world. A short, specific brief per account is worth more than a clever template, because the specificity is what earns a reply.

This is the part people skip because it feels slow. It is also the part that makes outbound work, so I built tooling to make it fast rather than skip it.

## Then run a real cadence

One email is a wish, not a campaign. A simple multi touch sequence respects how people actually decide. Open with a specific observation about them. Follow up with a different angle a few days later. Add a short message on the channel they live on. Close with a friendly note that leaves the door open. Five touches across a week or two, each grounded in something real, will out perform a single perfect email every time.

## Keep what works

Treat the first two weeks as data collection. Watch which openers get replies and which die. Feed that back into the next batch. The pipeline you build in month one is rough. The one you build in month three, after the feedback has compounded, is the one that pays.

Starting from zero is not a disadvantage. It forces you to earn every account with relevance instead of buying reach. That habit is what makes the whole motion durable.
