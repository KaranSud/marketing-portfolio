# Marketing Budget Allocation Is Backing the Wrong Bet

Gartner shows spend chasing new logos while McKinsey shows retention is what actually pays.

[HERO IMAGE HERE]

Every CMO I talk to can tell you exactly what awareness and conversion spend bought them this quarter, and almost none of them can tell you what retention spend would have bought instead. That asymmetry is not an accident of reporting. It is the direct result of where the budget has been moving for two straight years, toward the customer who does not exist yet and away from the one already paying the bill.

## The Acquisition Reflex Is Eating the Budget

I keep seeing the same instinct play out in budget season after budget season. Pipeline softens, the board asks for a growth plan, and the easiest lever to pull is always the same one: spend more to bring in new logos. Nobody walks into a budget review asking to cut the demand gen line. Plenty of people walk in asking to cut the loyalty program, the lifecycle team, or the renewal content nobody on the leadership team reads closely enough to defend.

This is not a story about marketers making a bad decision in isolation. It is a story about marketing budget allocation as a process that rewards the function everyone can see on a dashboard and quietly starves the function whose payoff shows up a year later as a renewal nobody had to fight for. Acquisition spend produces a number this quarter. Retention spend produces a number that shows up as the absence of a problem, and absence of a problem has never once impressed a board.

I have sat through enough of these reviews to recognize the rhythm. Someone presents a pipeline chart with a gap between the number and the target, and within ten minutes the conversation has moved to which channel needs another six figures to close it. Nobody in that room is asking why the existing customer base, the one already paying and already convinced, is not generating enough expansion revenue to close the same gap for less money and less risk. The acquisition line gets the benefit of the doubt because it is the line everyone already knows how to defend in front of finance.

## Gartner Just Quantified the Shift

The instinct is no longer just a hunch. Gartner's 2026 CMO Spend Survey found that [awareness and conversion now account for 62.6 percent of total media spend](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-06-08-gartner-marketing-survey-finds-awareness-and-conversion-account-for-62-6-of-total-media-spend), a rise of more than ten percent since 2024, while spending on customer loyalty and retention has fallen 29 percent over the same period to less than 15 percent of the total. Two years ago this was a tilted budget. Today it is closer to a one-way street, with more than four dollars going toward acquisition for every dollar going toward keeping the customers a company already has.

A four to one ratio sounds abstract until it gets translated into headcount and campaign calendars. It means the team building the next paid search push outnumbers the team building the next renewal campaign four to one, even though the renewal campaign is protecting revenue the company has already booked and the paid search push is chasing revenue it has not earned yet. It means the lifecycle marketer asking for a modest budget increase to fund a churn-reduction sequence is competing for the same dollar as a demand generation manager who can point to a pipeline number on a slide, and the slide with the bigger number usually wins the room.

Read that shift next to the rest of the survey and the picture gets more uncomfortable. The same CMOs increasing acquisition spend are not seeing acquisition results improve to match. Gartner found that 20 percent of CMOs admitted their department fell short of its customer acquisition goals in 2026, up from 13 percent the year before, while only 15 percent missed their retention objectives. The function getting the budget increase is the one missing its targets more often, and the function losing budget every year is still hitting its numbers more reliably than the one replacing it.

## The Valuation Math Nobody Puts in the Board Deck

Budget decisions rarely get tested against what a buyer would actually pay for the business those decisions produce. McKinsey ran that test directly. Its analysis of more than 100 B2B SaaS companies between 2019 and 2024 found that [companies in the top quartile of net revenue retention traded at a median 24x EV to revenue multiple, compared with 5x for bottom quartile peers](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-net-revenue-retention-advantage-driving-success-in-b2b-tech), with top performers posting 113 percent retention against 98 percent for the laggards. A 15 point gap in one retention metric is showing up as a nearly 5x gap in what the market is willing to pay for the same revenue.

Picture two companies booking the same annual revenue this year. One keeps losing ground inside its existing customer base and refills the gap with new logos bought at rising acquisition cost. The other expands inside the accounts it already has and spends comparatively little to do it. McKinsey's data says the market will not value those two businesses anywhere close to the same multiple, even though this year's revenue line looks identical on paper. The premium is not paid for growth. It is paid for where the growth came from.

This is the number that should be reshaping marketing budget allocation and largely is not. A board will sit through a presentation about brand lift or share of voice without asking what multiple it buys. The same board, if it ever saw retention plotted against valuation this directly, would have a much harder time waving through another acquisition increase without first asking what the retention number is doing and why nobody funded it to move.

## The Growth Gap Between Leaders and Laggards Is Not Subtle

If retention quietly compounded into a modest advantage, the budget tilt toward acquisition would be a reasonable trade. It does not compound modestly. McKinsey's 2026 Global B2B Pulse Survey, drawing on nearly 4,000 decision makers across 13 countries, found that [60 percent of self-identified market leaders report double-digit revenue growth, compared with 21 percent of laggards](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/growth-marketing-and-sales/our-insights/the-surprising-economics-of-b2b-growth-the-new-survival-threshold-and-what-it-takes-to-thrive). That is not a narrow edge between similar companies executing the same playbook with slightly different discipline. It is a three times gap in the single growth metric every board cares about most, and the companies on the winning side of it are not the ones pouring an ever larger share of budget into the top of the funnel.

The pattern across both McKinsey findings lines up cleanly. Customer acquisition cost keeps climbing for every company chasing the same shrinking pool of net new logos, while the leaders pulling ahead are the ones whose existing customers keep expanding without a comparable new spend. Acquisition is not free of returns. It is increasingly a return that costs more every year to produce, while the retention side of the ledger keeps paying out at a flat or improving cost.

## What CMOs Say They Want vs Where the Money Actually Goes

Ask a CMO directly whether retention matters and almost none will say no. Marketing ROI conversations in nearly every boardroom I have sat in this year include a slide about lifetime value, a slide about churn, and a nod toward customer success as a strategic partner. Then the next budget cycle arrives and the dollars move exactly the way Gartner's survey describes, further toward acquisition and further away from the function everyone just agreed mattered.

This gap between stated priority and actual spend is not hypocrisy. It is a structural bias in how marketing gets measured. Acquisition spend ties to a number that updates weekly and survives a single quarter's scrutiny. Retention spend ties to a number that takes a full renewal cycle to prove out, and most budget conversations do not wait that long before reallocating toward whatever produced a visible result last month. The team optimizing for the metric that reports fastest will always look more justified in front of a finance partner, even when the slower metric is the one carrying the valuation.

Part of the problem is organizational. Acquisition usually reports up through a demand generation leader whose entire job is sized against pipeline contribution, while retention content, when it exists at all, often gets built by whichever lifecycle marketer has spare capacity that month, reporting into a customer marketing function that nobody on the leadership team considers a growth driver. Two functions producing wildly different valuation outcomes are frequently staffed, measured, and defended as if one of them is core to the business and the other is a courtesy. Gartner's own goal-miss numbers suggest that ranking has it backward.

## What Marketing Teams Should Actually Build

[INFOGRAPHIC HERE]

Fixing this does not require flipping the budget split overnight or starving acquisition of the spend it still legitimately needs to replace natural churn and enter new markets. It requires treating retention spend as a growth investment with its own forecast, its own dashboard, and its own seat in the same budget conversation that currently belongs to acquisition by default. A lifecycle campaign aimed at expansion inside an existing account should be evaluated against the same return bar as a paid acquisition campaign, not against a vaguer standard of customer satisfaction that nobody on the finance team can size.

Start by giving retention spend a number that updates as often as the acquisition dashboard does. Revenue retained from existing accounts, expansion pipeline, and renewal risk by account tier can all be tracked weekly with the same rigor as cost per lead, and putting them on the same slide as acquisition metrics is the single fastest way to stop retention from losing every budget fight by default. Then build the content the expansion motion actually needs: a renewal-readiness brief for account teams, an in-product education series for underused features tied directly to churn risk, and a quarterly business review template that gives a champion something concrete to bring to their own renewal conversation.

A useful test for any marketing leader walking into next year's planning cycle: pull the last four quarters of budget requests and sort them by which side of the ledger they fed. If acquisition requests outnumber retention requests by anything close to the four to one split Gartner found at the industry level, the team is not allocating budget on the basis of where the return actually lives. It is allocating budget on the basis of which function asks first and asks loudest, and that is a planning process, not a strategy.

None of this asks marketing to abandon the new logo. It asks marketing to stop treating the existing customer as a finished deal that runs itself. Fund the customer who already trusts you as deliberately as you fund the stranger who has never heard of you, and the budget finally matches the math.
