1/ The paid prospecting stack is confidently wrong about a third of your targets.

And you're paying $400-500/month for the privilege.

I stopped paying and built my own instead. Here's exactly why:

2/ B2B contact data decays at 2.1% per month under normal conditions.

In fast-moving sectors, that hit 3.6% monthly in late 2024.

That means ~30% of any database can go stale in a year even if it was accurate when you bought it.

3/ The paid tools don't tell you when a record was last verified.

They print a revenue figure — "$8M to $12M" — with the same visual confidence as a verified phone number.

No source. No timestamp. Just confidence.

4/ Sales reps working from bad enrichment data waste ~500 hours a year validating it manually.

That's 62 full workdays.

You're paying the subscription AND paying in time to check if the subscription was right.

5/ Here's what a lean outbound stack actually costs:

Mid-tier Apollo: ~$100/mo per user
Add intent data: $400-500/mo
Add sequencing + deliverability tools: more

A 5-person SDR team averages $47K-156K/year on tools. Using ~60% of what they pay for.

6/ The bigger problem no one talks about: these tools were built for B2B tech.

Point one at a restaurant group, a law firm, or a retail brand and the data thins out fast.

The messaging defaults to software language. Restaurant owners notice immediately.

7/ So I built from scratch instead.

My tool classifies the business type first, then pulls signals relevant to that type:
- Reviews and coverage for restaurants
- Catalog and repeat purchase signals for retail
- Case study language for agencies

Then it writes outbound that talks to the actual business.

8/ The constraint I built in by design: it cannot fabricate numbers.

Every fact traces to a public source I can open in a browser.

If a revenue figure isn't findable, the field stays blank. No confident estimates. No interpolated headcounts.

9/ This matters more than it sounds.

Cold email reply rates have dropped from 8.5% in 2019 to roughly 3-5% entering 2026.

A major reason: buyers have learned to recognize fabricated personalization. They can feel when the "research" is a guess.

10/ The honest tradeoff: my tool has lower contact coverage than Apollo per query.

If I need 500 CFOs at mid-market logistics companies, a paid database gets there faster.

I'm not hiding that. It's the real cost of the approach.

11/ What the build gave me that a subscription can't:

Works for any business type.
Zero running cost.
Never claims what it doesn't know.
Forces you to understand every assumption the paid tools make implicitly.

12/ That last one is underrated.

Building the engine made me understand which signals actually indicate intent, how to classify a business without Crunchbase, how to find a decision-maker without LinkedIn Sales Navigator.

That judgment stays with you after the tool becomes obsolete.

13/ Build vs buy is never truly binary.

The question is: what are you optimizing for, and what does the subscription actually give you for the price?

For a 20-person sales team with a tight ICP inside B2B tech: buy. It's worth it.

For a lean operation testing a motion across business types: the math flips.
